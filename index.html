Standardizing Measures and Practices in Psychedelic Science

The StaMPS Data Framework is an international expert-consensus project developing a standardized, purpose-sensitive framework for what data should be collected in psychedelic medicine — and when. Its central product is an interactive tool that recommends Core and Supplementary data domains tailored to each user's context.

Why standardize data collection in psychedelic medicine?

Psychedelic therapies are complex interventions: a pharmacological agent is delivered within a psychosocial protocol, shaped by preparation, support, set and setting, and follow-up care. As these treatments move from trials toward broader implementation, the data collected across studies, clinics, and jurisdictions remain strikingly heterogeneous. Different sites measure different constructs, with different instruments, at different timepoints — limiting comparability, hampering evidence synthesis, and slowing sound clinical, regulatory, and policy decisions.

Existing reporting guidelines each cover part of the picture — trial reporting (CONSORT and its extensions), regulatory conduct (Good Clinical Practice, FDA guidance), intervention description (TIDieR, WIDER), set and setting (ReSPCT), health economics (CHEERS, CReDECI 2), and group-based interventions (GB-BCI) — but none was designed for the full scope of psychedelic care, and no consensus exists on which of their elements are essential in this field.

The StaMPS Data Framework addresses this gap by convening an international expert panel to define, through structured consensus, the Core data domains (the minimum essential set whose absence would raise serious concerns) and Supplementary domains (valuable but not strictly necessary) for psychedelic medicine — spanning intervention characteristics, participant characteristics, baseline assessment, safety monitoring, treatment experiences, and outcomes, rather than outcomes alone.

Purpose-sensitive by design

A central premise of the project is that the right data depend on why they are collected. Experts strongly endorsed (mean appropriateness 6.5/7) three primary purposes of data collection, which anchor the framework:

Clinical

Guiding Therapeutic Practices. Data collected to directly improve the safety and effectiveness of psychedelic therapy practices, for both well populations and patients.

Academic

Academic Research & Knowledge Development. Data collected to advance scientific knowledge on psychedelic drug effects, mechanisms, efficacy, and safety.

Economic

Economics & Health Policy. Data collected to inform policy decisions regarding access, regulation, funding, and insurance coverage.

The consensus process has likewise favoured conditional, modular recommendations over one-size-fits-all mandates: for example, experts preferred that group-intervention items become Core only when therapy is delivered in groups, and that health-economic items be Core for economic purposes only. The interactive tool reflects these specifiers directly.

Study design

The project uses a modified Delphi consensus methodology adapted from Core Outcome Set (COS) development, with four planned rounds:

RoundFocusStatus
Round 1Open elicitation: purposes of data collection, timepoint definitions, key design factors, and candidate Core/Supplementary domains from expert free-text responses.Complete
Round 2Guideline harmonization: six structured proposals for classifying items aligned with 12 established clinical and research guidelines.Complete
Round 3Item-level ratings of the many remaining candidate items without a Core verdict, refined by purpose and timepoint.Upcoming
Round 4Final consensus, thresholds, and ratification of the StaMPS Data Framework and interactive tool.Upcoming

Data-collection timepoints follow the expert-endorsed "By Timing" categorization — Outset, Baseline, During Treatment, and Post-Treatment — selected in Round 1 from four alternatives (mean usefulness 5.96/7; preferred by ~44% of experts).

A living, preliminary framework Current verdicts reflect consensus after two of four rounds. Items marked "Core" have strong preliminary support; the many items without a Core verdict have not been rejected — most simply remain to be evaluated in Rounds 3–4. Verdicts, thresholds, and item wording may all change as the consensus process concludes.

Help shape the framework

We actively welcome feedback from researchers, clinicians, policymakers, people with lived experience, and other stakeholders — on the framework's content, the tool's usability, and anything we may have missed.
